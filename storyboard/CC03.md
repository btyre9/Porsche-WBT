Step-Body-3: A slight forward lean — not invading personal space | Signal: "I am interested in what you are telling me" | Avoid fidgeting or appearing impatient

## Slide 09 — Tier 2: Active Listening
Slide-ID: SLD_CC03_009
Template-ID: content-tab
Slide-Title: Active Listening: Four Techniques That Change Everything
Voiceover-INTRO: The second tier of listening is active listening — and it goes well beyond just staying quiet while the customer talks. Active listening is a set of four techniques that ensure you and the customer share the same understanding. Explore each one.
Tab-Title-Paraphrase: Paraphrase
Tab-Body-Paraphrase: Re-phrasing what the customer said in your own words | Confirms understanding and shows you're processing, not just hearing | Not parroting — genuine restatement | Starters: "What I hear you saying is..." or "So if I understand correctly..." | Example: Customer: "It makes a grinding noise when I turn." You: "So you're hearing a grinding sound specifically during turns — not when you're going straight?"
Voiceover-TAB-Paraphrase: Paraphrase means re-stating what the customer said in your own words. Not repeating it back word-for-word — genuinely restating it. This confirms that what you heard matches what they meant, and it signals that you were actually processing their description.
Tab-Title-Clarify: Clarify
Tab-Body-Clarify: Asking for additional detail to remove ambiguity | Critical for accurate diagnosis — vague descriptions lead to missed problems | Example: Customer: "It just feels weird." You: "Can you help me understand what you mean by 'weird'? Is it a vibration, a pull to one side, a sound?"
Voiceover-TAB-Clarify: Clarifying is how you turn imprecise descriptions into diagnostic data. When a customer says 'it just feels weird,' that's not something you can work with yet. Your job is to help them find more specific language — and a well-placed clarifying question does exactly that.
Tab-Title-PerceptionCheck: Perception-Check
Tab-Body-PerceptionCheck: Confirming that both of you understand the same way | Catches misunderstandings before they cause problems or approvals that don't hold | Example: Customer says "That sounds expensive." You: "When you say expensive, what range are you thinking about? That helps me understand what options to present."
Voiceover-TAB-PerceptionCheck: A perception-check is used when a word or phrase could mean different things. It's not arguing — it's confirming alignment. 'That sounds expensive' could mean fifty dollars or five hundred dollars. Before you respond to an objection that may not even be the real concern, find out what the customer actually means.
Tab-Title-Summarize: Summarize
Tab-Body-Summarize: Recapping everything discussed before moving forward | Confirms nothing was missed; signals you heard the complete picture | Example: "Let me make sure I have this right. You're hearing a grinding noise when turning left, it started about a week ago, and it's been getting progressively worse. Is that correct?"
Voiceover-TAB-Summarize: Summarizing is how you close the listening loop before taking action. It signals to the customer that you heard everything — not just the first thing they said. And it gives them one final chance to correct or add anything before you proceed. Together, these four techniques form a system. Paraphrase to confirm understanding. Clarify to get specifics. Perception-check when a term could mean different things. Summarize before you take action.

## Slide 11 — Tier 3: Reflective Listening
Slide-ID: SLD_CC03_011
Template-ID: content-split
Slide-Title: Beyond the Words: Acknowledging What the Customer Feels
Image-File: CC03_reflective_listening.jpg
Col-Left-Header: What Reflective Listening Is
Col-Left-Body: Acknowledging the emotional content of what a customer says — not just the facts | Empathize: "I've been in a similar situation" — when you genuinely relate | Sympathize: Acknowledge their mood or feeling without needing to have shared it | When it's critical: expensive or unexpected repairs | Recurring problems causing inconvenience | Customer frustration with vehicle performance | Example: "I can see this has been really frustrating — let's figure this out together." | Example: "I understand this is a significant investment and you want to be completely sure."
Col-Right-Header: What Reflective Listening Is NOT
Col-Right-Body: Agreeing that something was done wrong | Accepting blame inappropriately | Making promises you cannot keep to make someone feel better | Performing empathy — be genuine, or don't do it | When a customer feels heard emotionally, they are far more likely to trust your recommendations and give you the benefit of the doubt
Voiceover-INTRO: The third tier of listening is reflective listening — and it's what separates a professional interaction from a truly excellent one. When a customer tells you their car has broken down for the third time, they're not just reporting a fact. They're frustrated. They may feel let down. They might be questioning whether they made the right choice. Reflective listening acknowledges that emotional layer. There are two forms. Empathy is when you can genuinely say 'I've been there' — you relate from experience. Sympathy is acknowledging their feeling even when you haven't shared it. 'I understand this is stressful' is sympathetic, and completely appropriate. Here's what reflective listening is not: it is not accepting blame for something that wasn't your fault. It is not making promises you can't keep. And it is absolutely not performing empathy without meaning it — customers know the difference. When a customer feels heard emotionally, they are far more likely to trust your recommendations, maintain their loyalty to the Porsche center, and give you the benefit of the doubt when something is complicated.

Voiceover-TAB-Mental: The most common mental barrier is planning your response while the customer is still talking. Accordion-Title-Environmental: Environmental Barriers

Accordion-Title-Mindset: Mindset Barriers
Accordion-Body-Mindset: Assuming the customer doesn't know what they're talking about | Dismissing imprecise descriptions as unhelpful | Impatience with non-technical language | Overcome: Customer descriptions, however imprecise, always contain useful information. Voiceover-TAB-Mindset: Mindset barriers are the trickiest, because they're internal. If you're dismissing a customer's description because it doesn't sound technical enough, you may be filtering out information that would actually help you. The customer who says 'it feels wrong' is telling you something — your job is to help them be more precise, not to dismiss what they're saying.
